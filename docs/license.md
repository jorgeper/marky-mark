## Why MIT and not something even more permissive

You asked for the most permissive license possible. Strictly, that would be
**0BSD** or **The Unlicense** (public-domain-equivalent — no attribution
required at all). Your dependencies do not prevent either: permissive deps
constrain how you *redistribute them*, not how you license *your own code*.

MIT is still the right call:

- It is the de-facto standard for exactly this ecosystem (React, CodeMirror,
  Vite, remark are all MIT) — contributors and companies evaluate it in zero
  seconds; 0BSD/Unlicense trigger legal review at some companies precisely
  because they're unusual.
- The only obligation MIT imposes on users is keeping your copyright notice —
  which costs them nothing and keeps your name on the work.
- GitHub, npm, and cargo all have first-class MIT metadata support.

If you truly want zero conditions, 0BSD is the drop-in alternative — everything
below still holds. My recommendation stays MIT.

## What we must do to comply (regardless of our license choice)

Distributing binaries means redistributing the dependencies inside them, so:

1. **`LICENSE`** at the repo root: MIT, "Copyright (c) 2026 Jorge Pereira".
2. **`THIRD-PARTY-NOTICES.md`**, generated by a script (`npm run licenses`),
   listing every shipped npm package and cargo crate with its license — this
   satisfies the MIT/BSD/Apache attribution requirements and the Apache-2.0
   NOTICE clause in one file. Attached to releases alongside the binaries is
   nice-to-have; in-repo is sufficient.
3. **Metadata**: `"license": "MIT"` in package.json, `license = "MIT"` in
   src-tauri/Cargo.toml, license named in the About dialog and README.
4. highlight.js (BSD-3) and diff-match-patch (Apache-2.0) need no special
   handling beyond appearing in the notices file.
